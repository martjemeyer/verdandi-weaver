The body before the story

A short contemplative on letting the nervous system speak first, before the mind hurries in to translate.

Spring · nervous systems · wholeness

There is a moment, very early, before the thought arrives.

You feel it as a small contraction, a tilt in the room, a softening in the chest. Then — quickly, almost without seam — the mind arrives with its sentence: I am anxious. I am unwelcome. I am too much. And the sentence becomes the weather we live inside for the next several hours.

But there was a moment before the sentence. The body knew something. It hadn't named it yet.

What if we waited

Most of what I have learned of healing is, simply, the practice of waiting. Not waiting for the feeling to leave — that is another contraction, dressed in patience. Waiting for the shape of the feeling to arrive in its own time. The body works in slower seasons than the mind.

The nervous system is not a problem to be solved. It is a place that has been asked, for a long time, to translate too quickly.

When we wait — really wait, with no agenda for what should appear — something quieter happens. The story we were about to grab onto loosens its hand. Underneath it, we find a sensation. Often only a few words long. Sometimes only a temperature. A direction. A weight.

The weather underneath

This isn't a method. It's closer to a permission. The permission to let the body be the first speaker, and the mind — beautiful, ancient mind — be the second. To translate carefully, instead of urgently.

What we have called fragmentation often begins here: the mind grabs the wheel because no one trusted the body to drive. Returning the wheel takes practice. It also takes faith — faith that there is, in fact, a body underneath the noise, and that it knows where it is going.

If something in this resonates, you might sit with the Burnout Scan — not to diagnose anything, but to listen further. Or read on, into The Ecology of Exhaustion, which holds this same thread at a different scale.
